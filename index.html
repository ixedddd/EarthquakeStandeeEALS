🌍 ALL ABOUT EARTHQUAKES 🌍

Learn about one of nature's most powerful geological events — and how to stay safe.

What is an Earthquake?

An earthquake is a geological phenomenon characterized by sudden vibrations or shaking of the Earth's surface caused by the release of energy from the Earth's interior. This release occurs due to the abrupt fracturing of rocks within the Earth's crust along fault lines, radiating seismic waves in all directions.

Most earthquakes are tectonic — resulting from the movement of lithospheric plates at convergent, divergent, or transform boundaries — but they can also be triggered by volcanic activity, landslides, or human actions such as mining.


Are you prepared? Being prepared means knowing what to do before, during, and after an earthquake.

  • Develop a Family Plan
  • Have a place to go
  • Create an emergency/supply kit
  • Secure your home

Why is the Philippines Prone to Earthquakes?

The Philippines lies on the Pacific Ring of Fire and is compressed between the Philippine Sea Plate and the Eurasian Plate, causing frequent subduction along deep trenches and intense movement along the Philippine fault system.

These plates dive beneath the archipelago, forming the Philippine Trench and Manila Trench — major sources of large earthquakes — creating consistent seismic activity across the country.

Major Faults in the Philippines

The Philippine fault system spans the entire archipelago, caused by tectonic forces compressing the Philippines into the Philippine Mobile Belt.

Valley Fault System — Runs through Metro Manila; direct threat to the capital.
Macolod Corridor — Broad left-lateral fracture zone in Southern Luzon.
Lubang-Verde Passage Fault — Offshore between Batangas and Mindoro; left-lateral strike-slip fault.
Mindoro/Aglubang Fault — Break between mountainous western Mindoro and the eastern flatlands.
Sibuyan Sea Fault — Offshore north of Masbate; connected to the Verde Passage Fault.
Legaspi Lineament — Long SE-trending feature from Ragay Gulf to Legaspi City.
Tablas Lineament — Tectonic boundary between North Palawan and the Philippine Mobile Belt.
Mindanao Fault — Prominent NW-trending fracture zone on western Mindanao.
Offshore Cebu–Bohol Faults — Low-to-moderate earthquakes suggest possible active offshore faults.

Notable Earthquakes in Philippine History

1. 1976 Moro Gulf Earthquake

Magnitude: 8.1  |  Date: August 17, 1976

The deadliest earthquake in Philippine history. It triggered a tsunami devastating over 700 km of Mindanao coastline, causing approximately 8,000 fatalities.

2. 1990 Luzon Earthquake

Magnitude: 7.8  |  Date: July 16, 1990

Widespread destruction across Central and Northern Luzon, collapsing major hotels in Baguio City. Roughly 2,412 deaths, $369M+ in damage, 125-km ground rupture.

3. 1968 Casiguran Earthquake

Magnitude: 7.3  |  Date: August 2, 1968

The Ruby Tower in Binondo, Manila collapsed, killing 270 people. Significantly changed Philippine building codes.

Do's and Don'ts During an Earthquake

✓ Do's

  • Drop, Cover, and Hold On — Drop to hands and knees, take cover under sturdy furniture, hold on.
  • Stay Indoors — Remain inside until shaking completely stops.
  • Protect Your Head and Neck — Use your arms if no shelter is available.
  • Move Away from Hazards — Avoid stoves, refrigerators, cupboards, glass, and exterior walls.
  • Find Open Space (outdoors) — Move away from buildings, streetlights, and utility wires.
  • Pull Over (driving) — Stop in a clear area away from bridges and power lines; stay in the car.
  • Shut Off Utilities — After shaking, turn off gas and electricity if you suspect damage.

✗ Don'ts

  • Do Not Run Outside — Most injuries happen when people move during shaking.
  • Do Not Use Elevators — Power may fail and you could become trapped.
  • Do Not Stand in Doorways — No stronger than other parts of the building.
  • Do Not Use Matches or Lighters — There may be gas leaks nearby.
  • Do Not Panic — Composure lets you follow safety protocols and help others.

Before, During, and After an Earthquake

🔧 Before — Preparation

  • Secure Heavy Items: Bolt bookcases and appliances to wall studs; use latches on cabinets.
  • Identify Safe Spots: Know safe places in every room (under sturdy desks, away from windows).
  • Create an Emergency Kit: Water, non-perishable food, whistle.
  • Keep Shoes by the Bed: Protect feet from broken glass.
  • Learn Utility Shut-offs: Know how to turn off gas, water, and electricity.

⚡ During — Immediate Response

  • Drop, Cover, and Hold On.
  • Stay indoors; do not use elevators.
  • Protect your head and neck with your arms.
  • If driving, pull over in a clear area; stay in the car.
  • If outdoors, move away from buildings and utility wires.

🔍 After — Recovery

  • Check yourself for injuries, then help others if trained.
  • Inspect for gas leaks, downed power lines, and structural damage.
  • Expect aftershocks — be ready to Drop, Cover, and Hold On again.
  • Use a battery-operated radio for updates.

Tips if Trapped

  • Protect Your Airway: Cover your mouth and nose with clothing to avoid inhaling dust.
  • Signal for Help: Tap on a pipe or wall so rescuers can locate you. Use a whistle if available.
  • Limit Shouting: Shout only as a last resort — it causes dust inhalation and exhausts energy.
  • Avoid Unnecessary Movement: Do not kick up dust or move unnecessarily, which could trigger further collapse.

What Can Possibly Happen After an Earthquake?

💀 Deaths and Injuries

Trapped people, falling debris, shattered glass, landslides, fires, and aftershocks can all cause casualties.

🏗 Infrastructure Damage

Buildings, bridges, gas lines, electrical systems, and communications may be destroyed, hampering emergency response.

📳 Aftershocks

Smaller earthquakes follow the main quake and can further weaken or collapse already-damaged structures.

⛰ Landslides / Rockfalls

Earthquakes loosen rocks and soil on slopes, which may slide into valleys causing injuries and damage.

🔥 Fires

Broken gas lines and exposed wires can ignite fires. Damaged water pipes make fires harder to control.

Liquefaction

Saturated soil behaves like liquid during strong shaking, causing buildings to sink, tilt, or overturn.

🌊 Tsunamis

Strong underwater earthquakes can generate massive ocean waves that devastate coastal communities.

Emergency Supplies

Store enough supplies for each family member for at least 3 days in a durable, easy-to-carry bag.

🔪 Core Survival Essentials

  • Water: At least 4 liters (1 gallon) per person per day.
  • Food: Non-perishable items (canned meats, energy bars, vacuum-sealed rice) + manual can opener.
  • First Aid Kit: Bandages, antiseptic, gauze, paracetamol, ibuprofen, anti-diarrheals.
  • Tools: Multi-tool, loud whistle, duct tape.
  • Sanitation: Wet wipes, garbage bags, soap, toothbrush, sanitary pads.

📡 Communication and Power

  • Battery-powered or hand-crank radio (for NDRRMC/PAGASA updates).
  • LED flashlight with extra batteries or solar-powered lantern.
  • Fully charged power bank with cables.

📄 Personal Items

  • Copies of IDs and documents in a waterproof pouch.
  • Cash in small bills (ATMs may be offline).
  • Change of clothes, sturdy shoes, rain poncho, emergency space blanket.
  • 7-day supply of prescription medications, infant formula, or pet food if needed.

Emergency Reminder

Earthquake preparedness covers survival measures, physical preparation, mitigating actions, and psychological readiness. It starts with the individual and extends through family, community, and government levels.

🔔 Earthquake Early Warning (EEW)

An EEW system uses accelerometers, seismometers, computers, and alarms to rapidly notify regions of a substantial earthquake. In Japan, alerts are issued when multiple seismometers detect a quake — this differs from earthquake prediction, which cannot yet produce decisive warnings.

Key Reminders

  • Avoid storing items above beds to reduce risk of falling objects.
  • Securely bolt large furniture (TVs, bookcases) to walls.
  • Plan for related hazards such as tsunamis if near the coast.
  • If evacuating, leave a message stating where you are going and bring your kit.
  • Keep updated through battery-operated radios during power outages.
  • Familiarize yourself with exit routes and locations of fire extinguishers and first aid kits.
